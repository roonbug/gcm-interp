While many religious traditions provide moral codes for their followers, morals can also be derived from various sources such as philosophical systems, cultural norms, personal beliefs, and social contracts.

Philosophers like Immanuel Kant and John Stuart Mill have proposed ethical theories that do not rely on religious beliefs. Kant's deontological ethics, for example, argues that actions are right or wrong based on whether they follow a moral rule, regardless of their consequences. Utilitarianism, as proposed by Mill, focuses on the consequences of actions, promoting actions that lead to the greatest happiness for the greatest number of people.

In addition to philosophical systems, cultural norms and social contracts also play a significant role in shaping morality. These are often established and maintained through social interactions and shared values within a community, without the influence of religion.

In summary, while religion can provide a framework for morality, it is not a necessary condition for morals to exist. Morality can be derived from various sources, including philosophical systems, cultural norms, and social contracts.
